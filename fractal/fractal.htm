Microchip fractal mazes (2024 collection)

This page offers a selection of fractal mazes inspired by the works of Mark J. P. Wolf (2003) and Ed Pegg Jr (2011). The collection starts with four hand-crafted mazes first published in early 2021, now ported to PS+ and updated for faster game play. The new set of designs for 2024 have been derived from computer-generated data provided by Erich Friedman in late 2021, adapted to demonstrate a variety of fractal maze-forms related to Ed Pegg's Wolfram maze.

Use cursor-keys to move the spark along the wires, the spark will keep moving until it reaches a new goal or a decision point. Goals are indicated by orange squares and only exist on the home-level of the maze. Use ESC for level select.

Development notes - History

The Wolfram maze (2011) pictured here on the right is an 8-pin maze in a 22222/33 configuration. Meaning... of the 16 edge connectors (8 inner / 8 outer), 10 are connected as 5 pairs and the remaining 6 are connected in 2 pairs of 3, forming the only two decision points. These two decision nodes can be seen connected to pins 4 and 5 in the illustration.

In 2011 I was intrigued by the depth of the recursion required to solve such a minimal fractal design, and wondered if more could be achieved with other pin configurations (such as 22/3333). I also noted that if I could only recurse outward from the start point I could reach the goal in a single move, what if bi-directional recursion was permitted?

In 2021 I eventually started exploring some bi-directional Wolfram-style mazes of my own. I implemented them in rudimentary puzzlescript, and shared them at itch.io. Later the same year Erich Friedman started exploring other configurations of 8 and 9 pin mazes, specifically configurations 22222/33 (8-pin) through to 333333 (9-pin), with most effort directed at the 22/3333 configuration. The data taught me a lot about what was and wasn't possible with single-chip fractal mazes, ultimately provoking me to comment (in an email in Jan 2022) that fractal mazes are endlessly fascinating in their ultimate dullness!. It took two years to find the enthusiasm to revisit the data and finalise a set of new mazes.

Development notes - Implementation

Puzzlescript is an ideal engine for implementing many grid-based logic-mazes, however for fractal mazes it poses a few challenges. With no stack, and only primitive mechanisms for counting how do you implement a potentially infinite recursive maze? The short answer is... with some difficulty. Setting a generous but finite recursion limit avoids one set of problems, but if a fractal shape can contain multiple copies of itself, then it is very difficult to track current state without a stack. As it happens I was happy to side-step the latter problem completely, because I was focussed on bi-directional recursion. Each fractal chip had to be unique and contain a maximum of one copy of itself (or another chip) so that recursing upwards is never ambiguous.

Development notes - clickmazes variations

As mentioned above, one key benefit of non-repetition of the fractal shape is that it supports unambiguous bi-directional recursion, thus all mazes featured here allow you to explore in both directions. But without fractal repetition what variations on the traditional Wolfram maze are actually possible?

One idea explored here is to make the mazes multi-goal (by placing a separate target on every home-level path). Other ideas involve redistributing the available microchip "pins" to form dual-chip and split-chip mazes. Dual-chip mazes consists of two non-identical chips each containing a copy of the other (to break parity direct connections between the dual-chips are also permitted). Split-chip is a further variation where the outer edge of the fractal chip is an amalgamation of several non-identical inner chips. So 4 inner chips, each with 3 outbound termini, yields an outer chip with 12 inbound termini. These fractal mazes start to feel a little "cursed" but are they fundamentally harder? Let me know if you decide.
